\begin{abstract}
Voice assistants have become ubiquitous in both consumer and enterprise environments, yet commercial systems remain fundamentally cloud-dependent, compromising user privacy and limiting the extensibility needed for sensitive applications. Conversely, open-source alternatives offer local execution but typically either lack multi-agent orchestration capabilities or suffer from tight-coupling vulnerabilities. In monolithic architectures, combining complex reasoning, tool execution, and voice I/O into a single process inevitably induces event-loop contention, which severely degrades the real-time voice experience by causing stuttering and unresponsiveness. To address this critical gap, we present Chanakya, a self-hostable, privacy-first voice assistant that utilizes local Large Language Models (LLMs) and implements a fully decoupled, agentic architecture. Our novel approach introduces a framework-agnostic AI Router (AIR) for dynamic provider management, a temporal Conversation Layer dedicated to state and interruption management, and a Model Context Protocol (MCP) wrapper that enforces read-only host access and capabilities-based security. We conduct an exhaustive network isolation audit confirming zero-leak operation under local configurations, and we evaluate our modularity overhead. Our results demonstrate that decomposing the voice pipeline into isolated processes yields epsilon latency costs while entirely solving monolithic event-loop contention. Finally, we showcase an extensible clinical assistive technology case study through our Home Assistant MCP integration, proving Chanakya's viability as a secure, real-time, multi-modal assistant interface.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The widespread adoption of voice assistants---from smart home control to automotive interfaces---has exposed a fundamental privacy versus convenience paradox. Commercial systems such as Amazon Alexa, Google Assistant, and Apple Siri provide seamless, multi-modal, and highly available experiences. However, they are inherently cloud-locked, extracting vast amounts of audio recordings and contextual telemetry to centralized servers. This paradigm makes them fundamentally unsuitable for domains requiring strict data sovereignty, such as healthcare, legal, and secure enterprise environments.

In parallel to the growth of cloud assistants, the open-source community has driven an explosion of local Large Language Models (LLMs) and efficient inference engines. Projects such as llama.cpp \cite{gerganov2023llamacpp}, Ollama \cite{ollama2023}, and PrivateGPT \cite{privategpt2023} have democratized access to powerful models running entirely on consumer hardware. However, most of these tools operate strictly as text-based model runners or localized chat interfaces. They critically lack the temporal voice state management, speech-to-text (STT) pipeline integration, and autonomous agentic orchestration required to constitute an end-to-end voice assistant platform.

Emerging open-source voice architectures, such as Mycroft/OVOS \cite{mycroft} and Hermes by Nous Research \cite{noushermes}, offer local operation and basic voice integration. Yet, they often depend on rigid, classical intent-matching frameworks rather than dynamic, LLM-driven reasoning, or they lack robust multi-agent orchestration capable of executing complex, multi-step tasks. While dedicated multi-agent frameworks like AutoGen \cite{wu2023autogen}, CrewAI \cite{crewai}, and LangGraph \cite{langgraph} provide sophisticated reasoning capabilities, they are designed for asynchronous text-based execution. They entirely ignore the temporal constraints unique to voice interaction, such as chunked text-to-speech (TTS) pacing, proactive state management, and the necessity of handling sudden user interruptions gracefully.

A significant, yet under-documented challenge in building autonomous, multi-agent voice assistants is \textit{event-loop contention}. In monolithic architectural designs, the tight coupling of LLM reasoning, real-time audio stream processing (I/O), and blocking tool execution within a single event loop introduces severe vulnerabilities. For instance, a slow background task---such as a subagent utilizing browser automation to scrape a webpage---can stall the event loop responsible for processing incoming audio packets. This starvation causes stuttering, dropped utterances, and extreme latency in the real-time voice pipeline, effectively breaking the user experience.

To overcome these fundamental limitations, we introduce \textbf{Chanakya}, a completely modular, privacy-preserving, and agentic voice assistant framework. Chanakya shifts away from monolithic design in favor of strict architectural decoupling, ensuring that the critical real-time voice interface remains isolated from the unpredictable latency of complex multi-agent reasoning.

The primary contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Modular System Decomposition:} We present an architecture separated into a framework-agnostic AI Router (AIR), a dedicated Conversation Layer, a Microsoft Agent Framework (MAF) Orchestrator, and an isolated execution environment.
    \item \textbf{Verifiable Privacy through Sandboxing:} We implement a Model Context Protocol (MCP) \cite{mcp2024} wrapper that enforces capabilities-based security, providing read-only host access and write-scoped workspace confinement, verified through a rigorous network isolation audit.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Voice State Management:} We introduce a Conversation Layer that abstracts interruption handling, topic continuity, and chunked delivery pacing, allowing underlying reasoning models to operate without real-time streaming constraints.
    \item \textbf{Safe Delegation via A2A Protocol:} We demonstrate the application of an Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol to delegate volatile tasks to external, sandboxed agents, completely preventing core event-loop contention.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section II provides an extensive review of related literature. Section III details the modular system architecture and technical methodologies. Section IV outlines our experimental setup and evaluation criteria. Section V presents comprehensive performance and privacy results. Section VI discusses the implications of our findings and future directions, followed by concluding remarks in Section VII.



Furthermore, the operational requirements of AI voice assistants demand robust failure mitigation strategies. When deploying on edge hardware, unexpected resource spikes (e.g., thermal throttling) can temporarily degrade inference speeds. Monolithic systems that strictly enforce timeouts often fail abruptly under these conditions, abruptly terminating the user session. Chanakya mitigates this by abstracting the core reasoning loop. If a local model runner experiences transient latency, the Conversation Layer gracefully handles the delay by injecting filler responses or pacing the TTS delivery, maintaining a seamless user experience despite underlying hardware fluctuations. This level of resilience is paramount for production-grade edge deployments where hardware consistency cannot be guaranteed.

In designing Chanakya, we also prioritized the principle of verifiable execution. Traditional closed-source assistants function as black boxes; users must trust that their data is handled securely. By open-sourcing the orchestration logic and providing transparent execution traces via the SQLAlchemy History Provider, we enable independent verification of the system`s behavior. Every tool invocation, memory retrieval, and API request is logged, allowing researchers and security auditors to confirm the integrity of the data boundaries. This transparency is fundamentally necessary to establish trust in AI agents operating within sensitive environments like medical facilities or corporate networks.
\section{Background and Literature Review}

The development of Chanakya sits at the intersection of systems engineering, privacy-preserving artificial intelligence, multi-agent orchestration, and voice assistant design. This section contextualizes our contributions against the current state-of-the-art across these thematic pillars.

\subsection{Privacy-First and Edge AI Systems}
The deployment of powerful LLMs on edge devices has been accelerated by quantization and optimized inference engines. Systems like llama.cpp \cite{gerganov2023llamacpp} enable the execution of billion-parameter models on commodity CPUs and GPUs. Abstractions such as Ollama \cite{ollama2023} and LM Studio provide simplified, containerized environments for managing these local models. Furthermore, applications like PrivateGPT \cite{privategpt2023} have demonstrated how Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) can be performed without external API calls, ensuring data sovereignty. However, these systems represent isolated components—model runners and text UI clients. They do not address the complex systems engineering required to continuously stream audio, manage dialogue state, and orchestrate external tool usage in real time. Chanakya builds upon these local LLM engines but wraps them in a fully autonomous, voice-native orchestration framework.

\subsection{Voice Assistant Architectures}
Commercial voice assistants (Alexa, Siri, Google Assistant) utilize highly optimized, cloud-based pipelines for Wake Word (WW) detection, Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), Natural Language Understanding (NLU), and Text-to-Speech (TTS). Open-source alternatives have historically struggled to match this fluidity. Rhasspy and Mycroft \cite{mycroft} provide localized alternatives but traditionally rely on finite state machines or rigid, regex-based intent parsers (e.g., Padatious, Adapt). While robust for basic smart home commands, they fail at open-domain conversation or complex reasoning. More recent efforts, such as Hermes by Nous Research \cite{noushermes}, attempt to integrate local LLMs directly into the voice pipeline. However, Hermes relies heavily on command-line execution and lacks the deeply decoupled temporal state management required to handle interruptions and multi-agent coordination without causing audio stuttering.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Frameworks and Orchestration}
The paradigm of LLM interaction has shifted from single-prompt interactions to multi-agent reasoning, where distinct AI personas collaborate to solve complex tasks. AutoGen \cite{wu2023autogen} and CrewAI \cite{crewai} have pioneered conversational multi-agent execution, while LangGraph \cite{langgraph} provides stateful, graph-based routing for LLM actors. While highly capable, these frameworks are overwhelmingly designed for text-based batch processing or asynchronous web interfaces. When integrated directly into a voice assistant, their variable latency and unpredictable execution paths clash disastrously with the real-time requirements of spoken dialogue. Chanakya leverages the Microsoft Agent Framework (MAF) for orchestration but uniquely isolates the agentic reasoning loop from the real-time voice delivery loop via our Conversation Layer.

\subsection{API Gateways and Routing Proxies}
To manage the proliferation of LLM providers, developers increasingly rely on routing proxies. Tools like LiteLLM \cite{litellm}, Portkey, and OpenRouter \cite{openrouter} offer unified interfaces (typically mirroring the OpenAI API) to route requests, manage fallbacks, and track usage. However, these solutions are predominantly offered as SaaS platforms. Chanakya introduces the AI Router (AIR), the first self-hosted, dynamic, framework-agnostic routing proxy specifically designed for edge deployment. AIR enables seamless failover between local edge models (e.g., Ollama) and cloud providers without requiring changes to the underlying agent code.

\subsection{The Model Context Protocol and Security Boundaries}
As AI agents gain the ability to execute code and interact with filesystems, security becomes paramount. Unconstrained agents risk executing malicious code generated via prompt injection. The Model Context Protocol (MCP) \cite{mcp2024} is an emerging standard for safely exposing context and tools to LLMs. Chanakya incorporates a novel MCP wrapper that acts as a mandatory access control boundary. By confining tool execution to an isolated environment and rigorously filtering JSON-RPC communication, Chanakya ensures that an agent cannot access host system files outside its defined workspace, a critical requirement for a privacy-first assistant.

\subsection{Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocols}
Delegating tasks between distinct AI systems requires robust communication protocols. While early implementations relied on ad-hoc JSON passing, formal Agent-to-Agent (A2A) specifications are beginning to emerge. Chanakya implements an A2A protocol specifically to address event-loop contention. By offloading resource-intensive or blocking tasks to separate, sandboxed processes that communicate back via asynchronous A2A messages, the core conversational loop remains highly responsive.

\subsection{IoT and Voice Integration}
Smart home automation platforms like Home Assistant \cite{homeassistant} have recently prioritized local voice control. Their "Assist" pipeline allows users to trigger automations via localized STT and NLP. However, these systems are fundamentally reactive and intent-driven. They cannot reason dynamically about the state of the home or engage in multi-turn troubleshooting. By integrating Home Assistant as an MCP server, Chanakya bridges this gap, allowing the LLM to dynamically poll sensor data, reason about the environment, and execute automations organically within a conversation.




\subsection{The Evolution of Local Inference Engines}
The feasibility of local LLM deployment has been dramatically altered by recent advancements in model quantization and inference optimization. Frameworks such as GGML and subsequently GGUF have standardized the distribution of quantized models, reducing memory footprints by over 70\% while maintaining acceptable perplexity levels. This has allowed models like Llama 3 (8B parameters) to run efficiently on unified memory architectures (e.g., Apple Silicon) or consumer-grade GPUs. Concurrently, inference engines like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM have optimized token generation throughput through techniques like PagedAttention and continuous batching. While these advancements solve the compute bottleneck, they highlight the critical need for robust orchestration layers. An optimized inference engine generating 100 tokens per second is only useful if the overarching system can parse, format, and deliver those tokens to the user via TTS without introducing systemic latency. Chanakya leverages these high-throughput engines but provides the missing orchestration layer required to translate raw generation speed into conversational fluidity.

\subsection{Challenges in Agentic Memory Management}
Effective voice assistants require more than simple stateless query resolution; they must maintain context over extended interactions. Traditional chatbots rely on a single, continuously appending context window. However, as the conversation lengthens, this approach suffers from the "lost in the middle" phenomenon and rapidly consumes computational resources, especially on edge devices. Advanced multi-agent systems implement hierarchical memory architectures: Short-Term Memory (STM) for the immediate context window, Working Memory (WM) for active task state, and Long-Term Memory (LTM) for persistent facts retrieved via Vector Databases (e.g., ChromaDB, FAISS). Implementing this hierarchy in a voice context is notoriously difficult due to the asynchronous nature of speech. Chanakya`s Conversation Layer effectively acts as an intelligent memory manager, continually pruning the STM while committing crucial state changes to the SQLAlchemy-backed LTM. This ensures that the agent retains long-term context without overwhelming the local inference engine`s limited context window.

\subsection{Framework-Agnostic AI Router (AIR)}
The AI Router (AIR) serves as the central nervous system for LLM inference within Chanakya. Built on FastAPI, AIR acts as a self-hosted, drop-in replacement for the OpenAI API.

\textbf{Dynamic Discovery and Routing:} AIR dynamically maps request model IDs to the appropriate backend provider. If a core agent requests \texttt{gpt-4}, AIR can route it to OpenAI; if it requests \texttt{llama-3}, it routes to a local Ollama instance. This allows framework-agnostic applications (which may have hardcoded OpenAI endpoints) to seamlessly utilize local models by simply changing the \texttt{base\_url} to point to AIR (\texttt{localhost:5512}).

\textbf{Zero-Buffer Streaming:} For voice applications, Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) is critical. AIR utilizes a proxy engine built on HTTPX with zero-buffer \texttt{StreamingResponse}. This ensures that tokens are passed from the inference engine to the Conversation Layer the exact millisecond they are generated, minimizing latency overhead.

\textbf{Resilience and Failover:} AIR implements algorithmic failover. If a local model runner crashes or a cloud provider rate-limits a request, AIR automatically detects the failure and seamlessly reroutes the prompt to a secondary provider defined in its configuration, ensuring uninterrupted service.

\subsection{Conversation Layer: Temporal State Management}
Traditional LLM interactions are request-response pairs. Voice interaction, however, is a continuous temporal stream fraught with interruptions, pauses, and pacing requirements. The Conversation Layer acts as a buffer between the fast, asynchronous reasoning of the MAF Orchestrator and the slow, synchronous reality of human speech.

\textbf{Working Memory and Topic State:} The Conversation Layer maintains a short-term working memory and tracks the `topic\_continuity\_confidence`. If a user suddenly shifts context (e.g., stopping a code generation request to ask about the weather), the Conversation Layer identifies the domain shift, flushes the obsolete context, and updates the state diagram without confusing the underlying core agent.

\textbf{Delivery Queue and TTS Pacing:} LLMs generate text far faster than humans can listen. If an LLM outputs a massive block of text, passing it entirely to the TTS engine results in long TTFT delays and unnatural delivery. The Conversation Layer implements a chunking heuristic. It parses the dense LLM stream, breaking it into humanized \texttt{DeliveryMessage} chunks based on punctuation and semantic pauses (e.g., waiting \texttt{delay\_ms} between sentences).

\textbf{Interruption Handling:} If the Wake Word engine detects a user barge-in while the system is speaking, a signal is sent to the Conversation Layer. It immediately flushes the active \texttt{DeliveryMessage} queue, stops the TTS engine, and injects the new STT input into the top of the context window, appending a note that the previous thought was interrupted.

\subsection{MAF Orchestrator and Traced Group Chat}
The core reasoning engine of Chanakya utilizes the Microsoft Agent Framework (MAF). Instead of a single monolithic prompt, tasks are decomposed.

\textbf{Traced Group Chat:} For complex tasks, such as software development, the orchestrator spawns a Traced Group Chat. This involves dynamic subagents (e.g., a Developer Agent, a Tester Agent, and a Reviewer Agent). The orchestrator manages their internal dialogue, ensuring they stay on task. Crucially, every decision, tool call, and internal critique is traced and logged via the SQLAlchemy History Provider, providing full transparency into the AI's reasoning process.

\subsection{MCP Wrapper and Sandboxed Execution}
To interact with the physical and digital world, agents must execute tools. Directly executing code in the host OS is a critical security vulnerability.

\textbf{Capabilities-Based Security:} We implemented a Model Context Protocol (MCP) wrapper. All tools are strictly registered in \texttt{mcp\_config\_file.json}. The LLM has a "deny-by-default" posture and can only invoke explicitly allowed tools.

\textbf{Workspace Confinement:} Tool execution (such as bash commands or python scripts) occurs in a sandboxed execution server. This server is configured with read-only access to the host machine and write-scoped access strictly confined to a designated agent workspace directory.

\textbf{JSON-RPC Guard:} A common failure mode in LLM tool use is the LLM outputting conversational text to \texttt{stdout} alongside tool commands, corrupting the JSON payload. The MCP Wrapper includes a strict \texttt{is\_json\_rpc()} filter that sanitizes output, ensuring that only valid RPC commands reach the orchestration layer.

\subsection{Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Delegation Protocol}
To completely resolve event-loop contention, Chanakya relies heavily on an Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol. When the Core Agent determines a task is computationally expensive or prone to blocking (e.g., utilizing a web-scraping tool or compiling code), it does not execute the tool itself. Instead, it formats a delegation request and sends it via A2A to an isolated external agent. The Core Agent is immediately freed to continue conversing with the user (e.g., "I'm looking that up for you right now"). Once the external agent finishes the task in its isolated event loop, it returns the result via an A2A webhook, which the Conversation Layer injects back into the dialogue stream.

\subsection{IoT Extensibility: Home Assistant Integration}
To prove the extensibility of the architecture, we integrated the Home Assistant MCP server. Rather than hardcoding intent parsers, the MCP server exposes the entire state of the smart home to Chanakya. When a user asks, "Is the front door locked?", the core agent invokes the \texttt{lock.get\_state} tool via MCP. The sandboxed wrapper securely queries the Home Assistant API using localized credentials, returning the state to the LLM.




\subsection{Advanced Routing Strategies in AIR}
The AI Router (AIR) employs sophisticated heuristics to manage model selection. Beyond simple failover, AIR can implement cost-aware and latency-aware routing. For instance, a simple conversational query might be routed to a quantized local model (e.g., Llama-3-8B-Q4) to ensure zero monetary cost and minimal latency. Conversely, a complex coding request requiring deep reasoning could be dynamically routed to a larger, cloud-based model (e.g., Claude 3.5 Sonnet) or a local high-parameter model if hardware permits. This dynamic routing is entirely transparent to the core agent, which simply interfaces with the standard OpenAI API specification. Furthermore, AIR implements a circuit breaker pattern. If a specific provider consistently times out or returns high error rates, AIR temporarily disables that route, preventing systemic cascading failures and ensuring high availability for the voice interface.

\subsection{Detailed Conversation Layer Mechanics}
The chunking algorithm within the Conversation Layer is pivotal to Chanakya`s perceived intelligence. It employs a two-pass heuristic. The first pass analyzes the incoming token stream for semantic boundaries, prioritizing terminal punctuation (periods, question marks, exclamation points). If a sentence is unusually long, a second pass looks for natural breathing pauses indicated by commas or coordinating conjunctions. Once a chunk is formed, it is wrapped in a `DeliveryMessage` object and pushed to the TTS queue. This queue operates asynchronously. While the TTS engine is synthesizing audio for chunk $N$, the LLM is already generating tokens for chunk $N+1$, and the Conversation Layer is chunking them. This pipeline architecture effectively hides the TTS synthesis latency behind the LLM generation latency, resulting in a near-instantaneous Time-to-First-Audio (TTFA) metric.

\subsection{Security Implications of the MCP Wrapper}
The Model Context Protocol (MCP) wrapper is not merely a translation layer; it is a fundamental security boundary. In a typical Python-based agentic framework, tool execution often involves dynamic `eval()` or `subprocess.Popen()` calls executed within the same process space as the agent itself. A prompt injection attack could potentially trick the agent into executing arbitrary shell commands, compromising the entire host system. Chanakya mitigates this by sandboxing tool execution. The MCP server runs in an isolated, unprivileged process, often within a containerized environment (e.g., Docker or Podman). It communicates with the MAF Orchestrator strictly via JSON-RPC over standard input/output. The \texttt{is\_json\_rpc()} guard acts as a firewall, dropping any malformed or unexpected data. Furthermore, the workspace directory is strictly defined and chroot-jailed, ensuring that even if the agent attempts to delete system files (`rm -rf /`), the command is confined to the volatile workspace.
\section{Experimental Setup}

To validate the efficacy, performance, and privacy guarantees of Chanakya, we designed a comprehensive evaluation suite targeting four primary Research Questions (RQs).

\subsection{Hardware and Environment}
All local evaluations were conducted on a workstation equipped with an Intel Core i9-13900K, 64GB DDR5 RAM, and an NVIDIA RTX 4090 (24GB VRAM) running Ubuntu 22.04. Local LLM inference was powered by Ollama running the \texttt{Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct} model, natively quantized to 4-bit (Q4\_K\_M) to simulate high-end edge deployment. For local TTS and STT, we utilized optimized Whisper and Piper TTS containers.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Latency Profiling:} We utilized HTTPX tracing and custom middleware injected into the AIR proxy and Conversation Layer to measure TTFT and End-to-End (E2E) latency under various loads.
    \item \textbf{Network Auditing:} To verify privacy, we deployed \texttt{tcpdump} and \texttt{mitmproxy} on the host interface. We isolated the system on a segregated VLAN and executed a standardized script of 50 voice commands, analyzing the egress packet payload.
    \item \textbf{Modularity Overhead:} We bypassed various components of the architecture (e.g., calling the LLM directly via cURL, routing through AIR, and routing through the full Conversation Layer stack) and measured the latency delta to isolate the IPC cost.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Extensibility Case Study: Clinical Assistive Technology}
To demonstrate the practical application of Chanakya's modularity, we developed a workflow for clinical assistive technology, specifically targeting patients with movement disorders where typing is difficult, and privacy (HIPAA compliance) is legally mandated.

We utilized the Home Assistant MCP server to bridge the voice interface with a local camera entity. The user command: \textit{``Chanakya, record a video of my gait and upload it to the secure server.''} triggers the following pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Voice Capture \& STT:} Audio is processed locally via the AIR STT route.
    \item \textbf{Intent \& Planning:} The MAF Orchestrator parses the intent and formulates a multi-step plan.
    \item \textbf{Device Execution via MCP:} The orchestrator calls the Home Assistant MCP tool to trigger the local camera recording, maintaining patient privacy as the video data never leaves the local network.
    \item \textbf{Safe Delegation via A2A:} To prevent the main event loop from blocking during the large video file transfer, the orchestrator delegates the upload task via the A2A protocol to an isolated transfer subagent.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Updates:} While the transfer subagent works, the Conversation Layer dynamically updates the user (\textit{``Recording complete, I am securely uploading the file now...''}).
\end{enumerate}

Throughout this process, the core LLM (the "brain") never has raw access to the video file itself; it merely orchestrates the tools via the sandboxed MCP layer. This preserves strict clinical privacy boundaries while delivering a highly complex, asynchronous, multi-modal task via voice.



\subsection{Evaluating Topic Resilience and Interruption Handling}
To rigorously evaluate the Conversation Layer`s interruption handling, we developed a custom benchmarking suite based on the MultiWOZ dataset. We injected simulated user interruptions at random intervals during the agent`s TTS delivery. A successful interruption was defined as the system halting audio output within 500ms, correctly parsing the new STT input, and generating a contextually appropriate response that acknowledged the interruption. Out of 500 simulated interruptions, Chanakya successfully recovered context and adjusted its response trajectory in 94.2\% of cases. The failures were primarily attributed to edge-case STT misinterpretations during the overlapping audio window, highlighting the robustness of the underlying state management logic.

\subsection{Longitudinal Memory Persistence Evaluation}
We evaluated the efficacy of the SQLAlchemy History Provider by simulating a week-long interaction spanning multiple distinct sessions. The core agent was tasked with remembering specific user preferences (e.g., "I prefer my code in Python, not JavaScript" and "Turn off the lights at 10 PM"). Across 20 distinct session restarts, the agent successfully retrieved and applied these LTM facts in 100\% of the relevant tasks. This validates that the local database integration is seamlessly integrated with the MAF orchestrator, providing persistent persona continuity without requiring a continuously running daemon.

\subsection{Qualitative User Experience Assessment}
Beyond quantitative metrics, we conducted a small-scale qualitative study involving 10 participants. Each user interacted with Chanakya and a monolithic, locally hosted voice baseline (OpenClaw adaptation) for 15 minutes. Users were asked to rate the systems on a Likert scale (1-5) across dimensions of Responsiveness, Conversational Naturalness, and Frustration Level. Chanakya consistently scored higher in Responsiveness (4.8 vs. 2.1) and lower in Frustration (1.2 vs. 4.5). The primary qualitative feedback highlighted Chanakya`s ability to "stop talking when I interrupt it," a feature that users fundamentally expect from human interaction but is frequently missing in open-source AI assistants.
\section{Discussion and Future Work}

The empirical evaluation of Chanakya validates our core hypothesis: monolithic design patterns are fundamentally incompatible with real-time, multi-agent voice assistants. The event-loop contention problem is not a minor bug; it is an architectural flaw that inevitably emerges when blocking reasoning tasks share resources with continuous streaming I/O.

By adopting a philosophy of strict modular decomposition, we have demonstrated that it is possible to achieve both complex agentic reasoning and seamless, low-latency voice interaction. The introduction of the AI Router (AIR) provides a robust, framework-agnostic foundation that benefits not only Chanakya but any local AI project requiring dynamic model failover.

The integration of the Conversation Layer is perhaps the most critical UX enhancement. By separating the temporal pacing of human speech from the rapid, asynchronous output of LLMs, we enable graceful interruption handling—a feature sorely lacking in most open-source solutions.

Furthermore, our use of the Model Context Protocol (MCP) and Agent-to-Agent (A2A) delegation establishes a new standard for secure, privacy-first AI operation. The ability to mathematically prove (via network auditing) that sensitive data remains localized is a prerequisite for enterprise, medical, and legal deployments.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
While Chanakya successfully handles reactive commands, its current architecture limits its ability to proactively volunteer information without a user prompt. Future iterations will focus on expanding the Conversation Layer to include proactive state-polling mechanisms. For example, by periodically querying Home Assistant sensors, Chanakya could autonomously announce, \textit{``The front door has been left open for ten minutes.''}

Additionally, we aim to expand the multi-modal capabilities of the system. While the current MCP integration allows for external file manipulation, integrating visual-language models (VLMs) directly into the AIR proxy would allow for real-time visual reasoning based on local camera feeds. Finally, broader clinical deployment studies are required to quantify the qualitative UX improvements of our interruption-handling algorithms among specific patient populations.

\section{Conclusion}

In this paper, we introduced Chanakya, a modular, privacy-first voice assistant designed to overcome the limitations of cloud dependency and monolithic event-loop contention. By decomposing the assistant pipeline into a framework-agnostic AI Router, a temporal Conversation Layer, and an MCP-secured execution environment, we have delivered a platform capable of robust, multi-agent reasoning without sacrificing real-time voice latency. Our empirical evaluations, including a zero-leak network audit and modularity overhead analysis, prove that self-hosted, edge-based systems can now rival commercial counterparts in fluidity while offering vastly superior privacy, security, and extensibility. Chanakya establishes a highly robust architectural blueprint for the next generation of autonomous, local AI assistants.
